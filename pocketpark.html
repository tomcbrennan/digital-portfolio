THE PROBLEM.

The ever-present prominence of parking congestion across Perth, accompanied by rising parking fees, marks a huge source of frustration for the average daily commuter.

PROJECT GOALS.

I aim to address this problem by bringing an unmatched, unique and satisfying digital experience when paying for parking. More specifically, in designing Pocket Park, I focussed heavily on tackling the following issues:

Help counteract the difficulty of finding a car park

Provide a source of emotional relief when reluctantly having to pay for parking

Promote reduced carbon emissions from driving

Key Features.

Clean, neumorphic approach to parking.

Standing out from the rest can be an overwhelming task. In designing Pocket Park, I wanted to take a more non-linear approach to the visual aesthetic. Neumorphism's modern and clean appeal caters to a simple application with only a handful of features. With users only using this app for minimal time, I wanted to ensure that the experience activating and de-activating parking was enhanced by this pristine visual approach and ease an of access to all features.

Activate your parking at the touch of a button.

Throughout the ideation process, I wanted to ensure that using the activation features took as little time as possible, allowing the user to spend more time doing what is most important in their day. This process needed to be seamless and provide as little distractions as possible, whilst also clearly providing the user feedback on the status of the action.

View available parking bays through logical navigation.

Before even paying for parking, finding a park can be source of immense frustration. Pocket Park aims to ease this frustration by providing quick feedback to the user on how busy their parking destination is and where they might find free parking bays within it using GPS positioning.

Split your fare with rideshare.

Parking fees are a nuisance. Enter ridesharing, whereby users are encouraged to drive one another to destinations, split the fare, and most importantly, pay less. The process is as simple as the driver scanning each riders QR code and the charge will be divided evenly. Quick and logical!

What did I learn?

Fundamentally, in designing Pocket Park I grasped just how important a clean interface is in regards to user satisfaction. Paying for parking is never an enjoyable process for anyone and that is something I continued to reiterate to myself when designing the application. As a result, I learnt that implementing satisfying user interactions such micro-animations plays a significant role in being able to turn a frustrating process such as paying for parking, into something that users gain an unexpectedly rich experience from.

On a more personal level, this project taught me the importance of patience and having a clear direction before iterating. Throughout the prototyping process, my direction on this project changed multiple times and I struggled immensely in how to approach the aesthetic of the interface. This was mainly due to how simple a parking app really is. Research as well as user testing played a vital role in me finally settling on a neumorphic style of approach that felt clean, compact and memorable.
